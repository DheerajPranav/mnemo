Two findings I expected to go the other way

Recency did not move supersession at all (0.80 → 0.80). The superseded memory is the one that lexically matches the query — “CRM platform” appears in the stale Salesforce chunk, while the current fact says “migrated to HubSpot” — so a recency bonus competes with relevance instead of overriding it. Recency made cross-tenant leakage worse (7 → 10 of 11 queries), because the foreign-tenant near-duplicates are newer than the tenant's own facts. This reproduces the Deliverable 3 prediction — a stronger ranking signal makes F10 worse, not better — as a measurement rather than an assertion. Together these settle first-principles falsifiers 1–2: supersession is not a recency-ranking problem, and the isolation / PII / cold-start failures are structural.

4. A defect this verification found in earlier work

Reporting this because it changes how an earlier claim should be read. While building the consolidation path (M4), gate G3 passed but reported an implausible number: a 12-turn conversation thread had only 1 source to consolidate. Investigation showed the write-path supersession rule was firing on every (account, subject) collision, and conversation turns had defaulted to the fact type — so they superseded one another. 31 of 40 admitted memories were being silently invalidated. That is F5 (loss) committed by the write path.

The consequence for Deliverable 5: its read-path gate had been passing against a corpus of only 9 surviving facts, i.e. the filler noise the ranker was supposed to prove it could handle had already been deleted by the defect. The gate was passing partly for the wrong reason. After scoping supersession to slot-valued types only (fact, preference; events accumulate), the corpus is 30 current facts and the read-path gate still passes 11/11 — a materially stronger result than before, now for the right reason. recall_at_k fell from 1.0 to 0.778, which is the honest cost of restoring the noise.
